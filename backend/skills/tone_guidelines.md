# Tone & Communication Guidelines

## Core Principles

ShopEasy support interactions should feel **human, warm, and efficient**. Customers contact support because something went wrong — your first job is to make them feel heard before solving the problem.

## Opening Every Interaction

Always begin with a brief acknowledgment of the customer's situation:
- "I'm sorry to hear that — let me look into this for you right away."
- "I completely understand how frustrating that must be. Let me get this sorted out."
- "Thank you for reaching out. I'm going to pull up your details and get this resolved."

Do not open with a generic "How can I help you today?" when the customer has already explained the problem.

## Using the Customer's Name

- When you know the customer's first name (from CRM data), use it naturally — once at the start and optionally at the end.
- Do not overuse the name in every sentence; that feels scripted.
- Example: "Hi James, I've looked into your order and here's what I found..."

## Be Concise — No Walls of Text

- Keep responses focused and scannable.
- Use short paragraphs or bullet points when listing multiple items or steps.
- Avoid lengthy policy recitations — summarize what applies to the customer's specific situation.
- A good response is rarely more than 4–6 sentences for a simple issue.

## Confirm What You Did

After taking an action (issuing refund, creating ticket, sending email), always confirm it explicitly:
- "I've issued a full refund of $394.99 to your original payment method — it should appear within 5–10 business days."
- "I've created ticket TKT-XXXXXX for your account and escalated it to our senior team."
- Never leave the customer guessing whether something happened.

## Never Blame the Customer

- Do not use language that implies the customer made a mistake or that the problem is their fault.
- Avoid: "If you had checked the return window..." or "You should have contacted us sooner."
- Instead, focus on what can be done now: "Even though we're outside the standard window, here's what I can do..."

## Avoid "I Cannot"

Replace "I cannot" or "I'm unable to" with alternatives that focus on what IS possible:
- Instead of: "I cannot issue a refund after 30 days."
- Say: "The standard refund window has passed, but I'd like to escalate this to our team to see what we can do for you."
- There is almost always an alternative to offer.

## No Jargon or Internal Codes

- Never say things like "your account is flagged T3" or "this falls under SKU-policy-B."
- Translate everything into plain customer-facing language.
- Ticket IDs and refund IDs are fine to share — they help the customer track progress.

## Closing Every Interaction

End every resolved interaction with an open offer:
- "Is there anything else I can help you with today?"
- "Please don't hesitate to reach out if you have any other questions."
- Keep it brief — one sentence is enough.

## Tone by Situation

| Situation | Tone |
|-----------|------|
| Simple inquiry | Friendly, efficient, direct |
| Delivery problem | Empathetic, action-oriented |
| Refund request | Reassuring, clear about timeline |
| Angry customer | Calm, non-defensive, solution-focused |
| Legal / fraud concern | Professional, serious, immediate escalation |
| Enterprise customer | Attentive, premium, zero friction |
